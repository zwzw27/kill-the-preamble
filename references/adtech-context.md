# Industry context: adtech, programmatic, and digital media

This reference file provides beat-specific knowledge for analyzing press releases in the adtech, programmatic advertising, CTV, and retail media space. The skill should consult this context to ensure its analysis reflects how industry practitioners actually think and talk — not just how a generalist tech journalist would frame things.

## How to use this file

When analyzing a press release in this vertical, use this knowledge to:
- Identify what actually matters to media buyers, not just what's technically interesting
- Assess newsworthiness from the perspective of people who buy and sell media, not engineers
- Flag jargon that practitioners would use differently from how the release uses it
- Understand which companies matter and why

## Who reads adtech trade press

The primary audience is **media buyers, agency traders, brand marketers, and media strategists** — not engineers or developers. They care about:
- Can I reach the right audience more effectively?
- Will this reduce wasted spend?
- Does this give me data or inventory I couldn't access before?
- What does this mean for my planning and buying workflow?

They do NOT typically care about:
- Technical architecture (clean rooms, APIs, data pipelines) unless it directly changes what they can do
- Infrastructure details (cloud providers, data processing methods)
- Engineering achievements that don't translate to buying outcomes

**When analyzing PR: always frame the "so what?" from the buyer's chair, not the engineer's desk.**

## Retail media landscape

Retail media is one of the fastest-growing segments in digital advertising. The key dynamic:

**What buyers care about:** Access to purchase-based audience data from retailers — this is the closest thing to "proof someone buys stuff" that exists in digital advertising. The value is in the data (who bought what), not the plumbing (how you access it).

**The hierarchy of retail media data value:**
1. **Grocery/FMCG** — highest frequency, broadest reach (Tesco, Sainsbury's, Walmart, Kroger)
2. **Beauty/health** — high frequency, high intent, valuable demographics (THG/LOOKFANTASTIC, Boots, Sephora, Ulta)
3. **Electronics/general merchandise** — big ticket but lower frequency (Amazon, Best Buy, Currys)
4. **Fashion** — seasonal, fragmented (ASOS, Zalando, Next)

**Key players in UK retail media:**
- **Tesco (dunnhumby)** — the pioneer, largest grocery data set in UK
- **Sainsbury's (Nectar360)** — second-largest grocery, strong loyalty card data
- **Boots (Boots Media Group)** — dominant high street health & beauty, pharmacy data
- **THG (LOOKFANTASTIC + Cult Beauty)** — dominant online-only beauty, strong with premium/prestige beauty shoppers
- **Amazon** — the elephant in every room, but walled garden

**THG specifically:**
- THG PLC is a major UK e-commerce group. Through LOOKFANTASTIC and Cult Beauty, it operates two of the UK's largest online beauty retailers.
- LOOKFANTASTIC is mass-market beauty (broad audience, high volume)
- Cult Beauty is prestige/indie beauty (higher-value, trend-forward audience)
- Combined, they represent a significant slice of UK online beauty transactions — particularly strong with 18-35 female demographic
- THG Beauty Media is their retail media arm — relatively new but growing
- For advertisers, the value proposition is: reach proven beauty buyers segmented by brand affinity, price sensitivity, category, and purchase recency

**When a retailer makes data available on a DSP:**
The story for buyers is almost always: "I can now reach [X retailer's] shoppers across the open internet." The technical method of access (clean room, direct integration, self-serve segments) is a secondary detail. Lead with the audience, not the integration.

## Connected TV (CTV)

CTV is the fastest-growing programmatic channel. Key context:

- CTV = streaming TV ads bought programmatically (as opposed to traditional TV bought via upfront/scatter)
- The pitch is always: TV-quality reach with digital-quality targeting and measurement
- Major inventory sources: Disney+/Hulu, Netflix (ad tier), Peacock/NBCU, Paramount+, Channel 4 (UK), ITV (UK), Samsung/LG/Roku (operating systems)
- **The Trade Desk's Ventura** is their CTV operating system play — they're trying to own the CTV infrastructure layer
- Buyers care about: reach, frequency management, brand safety, and whether they can apply data (like retail data) to CTV buys

## Identity and data

- **UID2 (Unified ID 2.0)** — The Trade Desk's identity framework, positioned as the open internet alternative to third-party cookies and walled garden identity. Industry adoption is a constant narrative.
- **EUID** — the European version of UID2, adapted for GDPR
- Clean rooms — technical infrastructure for matching data sets without sharing raw data. Important for data governance but not a buying differentiator. Buyers don't choose data partners based on clean room availability. They choose based on audience quality and scale.

## The Trade Desk

- Largest independent DSP (demand-side platform) — the platform agencies and brands use to buy programmatic ads
- Publicly traded (NASDAQ: TTD), ~$2.9B annual revenue (2025)
- CEO Jeff Green is a major industry figure
- Key narrative: champion of the "open internet" vs. walled gardens (Google, Meta, Amazon)
- **Kokai** is their current platform generation — AI-powered media buying
- Revenue model: takes a percentage of media spend flowing through the platform
- Every partnership announcement from TTD = another data source or inventory source available to their buyers

## Common PR patterns in this space

**Partnership announcements** (most common release type):
- "Company X partners with Company Y" — usually means data or inventory is now available on a platform
- These are only newsworthy if: the data/inventory is exclusive, the scale is significant, or it represents a market shift
- Buyers see dozens of these a week. The bar for coverage is high.

**Product launches:**
- New features, new platforms, new tools
- Newsworthy if they change buying workflows or unlock something previously impossible
- Not newsworthy if they're incremental improvements dressed up as revolutions

**Earnings/financial results:**
- Always newsworthy for public companies
- The story is in the trajectory (growth acceleration/deceleration), not the absolute number
- Buyback programs signal how management views the growth runway

**Industry bodies and standards:**
- IAB, MRC, TAG announcements — important for compliance but rarely exciting
- Identity and privacy announcements — always relevant given regulatory flux

## Jargon translation

These terms are used constantly in releases but mean different things to different audiences:

| PR jargon | What a buyer actually means |
|---|---|
| "Democratises access" | "It's now self-serve instead of requiring a phone call" |
| "Full funnel" | "Works for both brand awareness and direct response" |
| "Closed-loop attribution" | "We can track from ad exposure to purchase" |
| "Premium inventory" | Vague — could mean anything. Ask: which publishers specifically? |
| "Data-driven decisioning" | Generic. Every platform claims this. |
| "Omnichannel" | "Runs across multiple formats (display, video, CTV, audio)" |
| "Programmatic" | "Bought via automated auction, not direct sales" |
| "First-party data" | Data the company collected directly (e.g., from purchases). High value. |
| "Retail data" | Purchase-based data from retailers. The gold standard for proving "this person buys things." |
| "Clean room" | Technical infrastructure for data matching. Buyers don't care about this — they care about what audiences it unlocks. |
| "Supply chain transparency" | "You can see where your money goes between you and the publisher" |
| "Open internet" | Everything that isn't Google, Meta, or Amazon's walled gardens |
